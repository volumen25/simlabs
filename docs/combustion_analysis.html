6.1 Theory

In Chapter 1, combustion efficiency was defined as the ratio of the burner’s capability to burn fuel completely to the unburned fuel and excess air in the exhaust. In this lab, we carry out a full combustion analysis.

Fossil fuels may be classified as solid, liquid, or gaseous. The vast majority are based on carbon (C), hydrogen (H2), or some combination of the two called hydrocarbons. During combustion, oxygen (O2) combines rapidly with C, H2, sulphur (S), and their compounds, releasing energy. Except for special high-temperature applications (e.g. oxyacetylene welding), the O2 required for combustion is obtained from air.

For engineering purposes, air is considered to have the following composition by mass:

Component Mass fraction
O2 23%
N2 77%

The proportions in which elements enter the combustion reaction depend on their relative molecular weights:

Element Symbol Molecular Weight
Carbon C 12
Sulphur S 32
Hydrogen H2 2
Oxygen O2 32
Nitrogen N2 28

6.2 Stoichiometric Combustion Theory

Complete combustion of a simple hydrocarbon fuel produces carbon dioxide (CO2) from the carbon and water (H2O) from the hydrogen. For a hydrocarbon fuel of general composition CnHm, the combustion equation on a molar basis is:

\[\text{C}_n\text{H}_m + y\,\text{O}_2 \rightarrow a\,\text{CO}_2 + b\,\text{H}_2\text{O}\]

The element balances are:

Carbon balance: \[a = n \quad \text{[kmol CO}_2\text{/kmol fuel]}\]

Hydrogen balance: \[2b = m \implies b = \frac{m}{2} \quad \text{[kmol H}_2\text{O/kmol fuel]}\]

Oxygen balance: \[2y = 2a + b \implies y = a + \frac{b}{2} \quad \text{[kmol O}_2\text{/kmol fuel]}\]

In practice, combustion occurs in air rather than pure oxygen. Nitrogen in the air may react to produce nitrogen oxides, and fuels often contain elements other than carbon and hydrogen. Furthermore, combustion is rarely complete — exhaust gases may contain unburned or partially burned products in addition to CO2 and H2O.

For further calculations, air is treated as a mixture of 21 mol% O2 and 79 mol% N2, with nitrogen acting as an inert gas. The stoichiometric combustion equation for CnHm in air then becomes:

\[\text{C}_n\text{H}_m + y\!\left(\text{O}_2 + \frac{79}{21}\,\text{N}_2\right) \rightarrow a\,\text{CO}_2 + b\,\text{H}_2\text{O} + c\,\text{N}_2\]

with the additional nitrogen balance:

Nitrogen balance: \[c = y\,\frac{79}{21} \quad \text{[kmol N}_2\text{/kmol fuel]}\]

Flue gas compositions are expressed as mole fractions in kmol of product per kmol of fuel.

6.3 Worked Example: Combustion of Octane in Air

Determine the stoichiometric air/fuel mass ratio and product gas composition for combustion of octane (C8H18) in air.

\[\text{C}_8\text{H}_{18} + y\!\left(\text{O}_2 + \frac{79}{21}\,\text{N}_2\right) \rightarrow a\,\text{CO}_2 + b\,\text{H}_2\text{O} + c\,\text{N}_2\]

Carbon balance: \[a = 8 \quad \text{[kmol CO}_2\text{/kmol fuel]}\]

Hydrogen balance: \[b = \frac{18}{2} = 9 \quad \text{[kmol H}_2\text{O/kmol fuel]}\]

Oxygen balance: \[y = 8 + \frac{9}{2} = 12.5 \quad \text{[kmol O}_2\text{/kmol fuel]}\]

Nitrogen balance: \[c = 12.5 \times \frac{79}{21} = 47 \quad \text{[kmol N}_2\text{/kmol fuel]}\]

The balanced combustion equation is:

\[\text{C}_8\text{H}_{18} + 12.5\!\left(\text{O}_2 + \frac{79}{21}\,\text{N}_2\right) \rightarrow 8\,\text{CO}_2 + 9\,\text{H}_2\text{O} + 47\,\text{N}_2\]

Air/fuel mass ratio — the molar mass of octane is \(8(12) + 18(1) = 114\) kg/kmol:

\[\frac{12.5\ \text{kmol O}_2}{\text{kmol fuel}} \times \frac{1\ \text{kmol fuel}}{114\ \text{kg fuel}} \times \frac{32\ \text{kg O}_2}{\text{kmol O}_2} \times \frac{100\ \text{kg air}}{23\ \text{kg O}_2} = 15.25\ \frac{\text{kg air}}{\text{kg fuel}}\]

Flue gas composition (molar basis):

Total kmol of flue gas = \(8 + 9 + 47 = 64\) kmol/kmol fuel

Species Mole fraction
CO2 \(8/64 = 12.5\%\)
H2O \(9/64 = 14.1\%\)
N2 \(47/64 = 73.4\%\)

Other fuel impurities complicate the calculation. Sulphur, for instance, typically combusts to sulphur dioxide (SO2). Ash — the noncombustible inorganic mineral content of the fuel — is assumed inert and is neglected in the calculations below.

6.4 Mass-Based Combustion Chemistry

For solid and liquid fuels, composition is usually reported on a mass basis from an ultimate analysis. The stoichiometric combustion reactions, written using molecular weights, are:

Carbon: \[\text{C} + \text{O}_2 \rightarrow \text{CO}_2 \quad\Longrightarrow\quad 12 + 32 = 44 \quad\Longrightarrow\quad 1\ \text{kg C} + 2\tfrac{2}{3}\ \text{kg O}_2 = 3\tfrac{2}{3}\ \text{kg CO}_2\]

Hydrogen: \[2\text{H}_2 + \text{O}_2 \rightarrow 2\text{H}_2\text{O} \quad\Longrightarrow\quad 4 + 32 = 36 \quad\Longrightarrow\quad 1\ \text{kg H}_2 + 8\ \text{kg O}_2 = 9\ \text{kg H}_2\text{O}\]

Sulphur: \[\text{S} + \text{O}_2 \rightarrow \text{SO}_2 \quad\Longrightarrow\quad 32 + 32 = 64 \quad\Longrightarrow\quad 1\ \text{kg S} + 1\ \text{kg O}_2 = 2\ \text{kg SO}_2\]

Since air contains 23.2% O2 by mass, 1 kg of O2 is contained in \(1/0.232 = 4.3\) kg of air (the stoichiometric mass of air), which in turn contains \(4.3 - 1 = 3.3\) kg of N2. For sulphur combustion in air:

\[1\ \text{kg S} + 4.3\ \text{kg air} \rightarrow 2\ \text{kg SO}_2 + 3.3\ \text{kg N}_2\]

6.5 Procedure

When fuel composition is given on a mass basis, follow these steps:

  1. Total O2 required — Determine the mass of O2 required to combust each constituent and sum the results. Subtract any O2 already present in the fuel.

  2. Stoichiometric air: \[\dot{m}_{\text{air, stoich}} = \frac{\dot{m}_{\text{O}_2\text{, required}}}{0.232}\]

  3. Total mass of combustion products — Calculate the mass of each product. For example, for a carbon content of 84.9%: \[m_{\text{CO}_2} = \frac{84.9}{100} \times 3\tfrac{2}{3} = 3.11\ \text{kg CO}_2\text{/kg fuel}\] Sum all product masses to obtain the total.

  4. Combustion product analysis by mass — Express each product as a percentage of the total flue gas mass. For example, if the total flue gas mass is 12.09 kg/kg fuel: \[x_{\text{CO}_2} = \frac{3.11}{12.09} \times 100 = 25.7\%\] Repeat for each constituent (H2O, SO2, N2).
